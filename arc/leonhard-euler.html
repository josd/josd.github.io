Answer (TL;DR)

Calinger’s book is a comprehensive, source-driven biography that ties Euler’s life to his astonishing scientific output; Princeton first issued the hardcover in 2015 (696 pp; ISBN 9780691119274) and later a 2019 paperback edition.[2], [7], [8]

Reason Why (Evidence)

Princeton catalog copy and endorsements emphasize it as the first real full-scale biography, drawing on Euler’s massive published works and correspondence; specs list 696 pages and 55 halftones.[2], [1]

Major venues reviewed the book: Nature called it a panoramic biography; the British Journal for the History of Science and MAA Reviews provide scholarly assessments.[5], [6], [4]

Princeton’s sample chapter confirms the historical framing and approach (Enlightenment context, life-to-work narrative).[3]

    Euler Track: Scope & Sources of the Life/Work

    1. Corpus size: Euler’s known works are indexed by the Eneström catalog (E1–E866).[10]
    2. Opera Omnia: The biography is grounded in Euler’s collected works and correspondence (dozens of volumes), a key reason for the book’s depth.[1]
    3. Fields touched: Catalog and reviews highlight contributions spanning analysis, mechanics/physics, astronomy, and number theory—set in their Enlightenment institutions.[2], [5]
    4. Primary-source hub: The Euler Archive provides open access to originals/translations and context that complements the biography.[9]
    Takeaway: Calinger’s narrative + the Archive’s corpus let readers connect Euler’s life (St. Petersburg, Berlin, return to Russia) to the sheer scale and diversity of his mathematics.[3], [9]

    Themes

    1. Life intertwined with institutions. Euler’s career arcs through academies and political contexts; the book embeds proofs and papers in that fabric.[3], [1]
    2. Scale of production. Reviews and Archive figures underscore an almost unparalleled output across mathematics and physics.[5], [10]

    Studies & Context

    • Scholarly reception: Reviews in MAA, Nature, and BJHS discuss scope, strengths, and historiography.[4], [5], [6]
    • Editions: Hardcover first published 2015; paperback released 2019 (same page count) with new ISBN.[7], [8]

    Glossary (quick reference)

    Eneström number
    Identifier E1–E866 assigned to Euler’s works in Gustaf Eneström’s catalog; widely used by historians and in the Euler Archive.[10]
    Opera Omnia
    Euler’s collected works; the biography draws heavily on this corpus and correspondence.[1]
    Euler Archive
    Online repository of Euler’s original publications, translations, and resources, maintained with MAA/University partners.[9]
