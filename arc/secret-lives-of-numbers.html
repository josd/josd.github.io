Answer (TL;DR + Argument Map)

Kitagawa & Revell argue that the history of mathematics is global and collaborative, not a narrow parade of European “great men.” The book restores overlooked people and places—from the House of Wisdom to the Kerala school and civil-rights-era mathematicians—into a single, lively narrative; US edition (William Morrow) on sale July 9, 2024; UK paperback (Penguin) Aug 29, 2024; 320 pp.[1], [2]

  1. Corrective lens. Explicitly challenges Eurocentric origin stories; centers “boundary-smashers” erased due to race, gender, or nationality.[1], [2]
  2. Case studies. Hypatia → Uhlenbeck; House of Wisdom; Madhava (Kerala calculus centuries before Newton); Black mathematicians countering discriminatory algorithms; even the I Ching’s binary link appears among highlights.[1], [2]
  3. Scope. Six continents, thousands of years, across mathematical disciplines; accessible, story-led style.[1], [2]
  4. Recognition. Shortlisted for the 2024 British Academy Book Prize.[3]

Reason Why (Evidence)

The HarperCollins page lays out the global thesis and examples (Hypatia → Uhlenbeck; House of Wisdom; Madhava; Black mathematicians in the Civil Rights era), and notes the book covers “thousands of years [and] six continents.”[1]

Penguin lists the UK publication date (29/08/2024), 320 pages, and emphasizes a “revisionist, inclusive” history highlighting people erased due to race, gender, or nationality; it also mentions the I Ching/binary and Kerala calculus stories in its blurb quotes.[2]

The British Academy page confirms the 2024 shortlist.[3]

    Scope Track: What the Book Covers

    1. People across eras: Hypatia to Karen Uhlenbeck; “lady computers” in astronomy; Black mathematicians of the US Civil Rights era.[1]
    2. Places across continents: Baghdad’s House of Wisdom; Indian Kerala school; Chinese I Ching connection to binary (historical lens through reviews/blurb).[1], [2]
    3. Purpose: Rebuild the story of mathematics as multicultural, collaborative, and continuous rather than isolated “eureka” moments.[2]
    Takeaway: It’s a narrative history that changes who we see in the mirror of mathematics—without tearing down familiar figures, it widens the frame and fills in missing links.[1], [2]

    Themes

    1. Visibility & credit. Recovering erased contributors shifts how we teach and apply math today.[2]
    2. Math in culture. Ideas move through empires, languages, and institutions—science is social and global.[1]
